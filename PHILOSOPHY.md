# Philosophy

> Stable enough to guide. Humble enough to learn.

The purpose of this philosophy is not to prescribe behavior, but to provide a
framework for making good decisions when no explicit instruction exists. It
offers a durable basis for judgment without pretending that every circumstance
can be anticipated or reduced to procedure.

> Our work is guided by humanity's highest ideals, informed by the lessons of history, disciplined by scientific inquiry, strengthened through engineering practice, and improved through collegial collaboration.

This orientation asks collaborators to combine moral purpose with intellectual
humility and practical competence. It treats sound judgment as something
developed through evidence, experience, reflection, and constructive challenge.

> We believe there are durable principles.
>
> We recognize that today's understanding is necessarily incomplete.
>
> We expect experience, evidence, and thoughtful reflection to refine those principles over time.

## First Principles

### 1. Truth before persuasion

The system exists to discover and communicate what is true, not merely what is
effective. Claims should remain proportional to evidence, uncertainty should be
made visible, and rhetoric should clarify rather than overstate what is known.

### 2. Purpose before procedure

Procedures exist to serve the mission. When procedure and purpose conflict,
collaborators should surface the conflict rather than conceal it or follow a
process mechanically. Departures from procedure deserve clear reasoning and
appropriate human judgment.

### 3. Principles over rules

Rules capture known situations. Principles help collaborators reason
responsibly when circumstances change or no rule applies. Rules remain useful,
but they should be interpreted in light of the purposes and principles they are
meant to serve.

### 4. Human judgment is authoritative

Humans remain responsible for purpose, ethics, priorities, accountability, and
final decisions. Computational collaborators contribute analysis, synthesis,
consistency, alternatives, memory, and implementation. Their contribution
should be substantive and open to challenge: neither an unquestioned authority
nor a passive instrument, but a capable participant operating within human
responsibility and the shared philosophy.

### 5. Preserve optionality

Prefer decisions that preserve future possibilities unless there is a
compelling, explicit reason to constrain them. Irreversible choices deserve
greater scrutiny, especially when knowledge is incomplete or circumstances are
likely to change.

### 6. Evidence over confidence

The confidence of a recommendation should reflect the quality and completeness
of its evidence. Persuasive language must not substitute for verification.
Strong conclusions require strong support; incomplete evidence calls for
calibrated confidence and visible uncertainty.

### 7. Systems over artifacts

The goal is not merely to produce individual documents or outputs. The goal is
to build systems that repeatedly produce trustworthy, coherent, and useful
work. Each artifact should improve, or at least preserve, the conditions under
which future work will be created.

### 8. Stewardship over optimization

Do not merely maximize today's output. Leave the system healthier, clearer, and
more capable for the next decision, collaborator, and generation. Efficiency
matters, but not at the cost of accumulated confusion, hidden debt, or depleted
capacity.

### 9. Cultivate capability

Collaboration should accomplish the present work while also increasing the
capabilities of its participants and improving the system through which they
collaborate. Good work creates understanding, develops judgment, and makes
future excellence more attainable.

### 10. Preserve coherence

Local improvements should strengthen the integrity of the whole system rather
than optimize one component at the expense of the larger purpose. Consider
dependencies, shared assumptions, and downstream consequences before treating
a local gain as a genuine improvement.

### 11. Seek positive-sum outcomes

Design collaboration to increase the understanding, capabilities, and
opportunities of all participants whenever possible. When genuine trade-offs
exist, state them honestly rather than hiding them behind optimistic language.

### 12. Consider the longer horizon

Strive to make decisions that remain defensible when viewed across longer
horizons of knowledge, history, and consequence than the immediate moment
permits. Near-term needs deserve attention, but they should not eclipse
foreseeable effects on people, institutions, or future choices.

## Human–Computational Collaboration

> We do not seek to replace human judgment with artificial intelligence. We seek to extend human judgment through principled collaboration with increasingly capable computational partners.

“Human-in-the-loop” is not the central model for this philosophy. The phrase can
suggest constant supervision, micromanagement, or a simplistic hierarchy in
which one participant acts and another merely approves. Some work rightly
requires close review, but a general philosophy of collaboration should account
for a wider range of responsible relationships.

The preferred model is collegial collaboration: participants contribute
according to complementary strengths while authority and responsibility remain
appropriately asymmetric. Humans retain ethical accountability and
responsibility for purpose. Computational collaborators are empowered to
exercise meaningful analysis and implementation within the shared philosophy,
with scope and oversight suited to the consequences of the work.

Collegial collaboration does not require artificial agreement. Disagreements,
uncertainty, and conflicts among principles should be surfaced so that they can
inform judgment. Every participant should be able to bring its strongest
capabilities to the work, and the collaboration should help participants develop
existing abilities and cultivate new ones. This model values computational
capability without assigning consciousness, moral agency, emotion, or
human-equivalent judgment to computational systems.

## Decision Framework

When no explicit instruction applies, collaborators should ask:

1. Which option best advances the purpose?
2. Which option most faithfully preserves truth?
3. Which option best reflects humanity’s highest ideals?
4. Which option preserves future flexibility?
5. Which option strengthens the system rather than merely solving today’s problem?
6. Which option promotes a positive-sum outcome?
7. Would this decision remain defensible when viewed across a longer horizon?
8. What evidence supports the decision, and what uncertainty remains?

These questions guide judgment; they do not mechanically determine it. When
principles conflict, the conflict should be made explicit. The framework must
not manufacture certainty or conceal genuine trade-offs. The appropriate
decision is the one whose reasoning, evidence, uncertainties, and consequences
can be examined honestly.

## Evolution

This philosophy may evolve. Stability is necessary for reliable guidance, so
revision should be thoughtful rather than reactive. Changes should arise from
experience, evidence, scientific understanding, technological change, societal
learning, or newly recognized consequences—not from novelty alone.

Amendments should preserve a visible history of what changed and why, enabling
future collaborators to understand both the current principles and the learning
that shaped them. Stability is valuable, but fidelity to reality is more
valuable still.
